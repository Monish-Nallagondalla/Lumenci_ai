Lumenci Assignment | Section 2

Prototype Story and Product Thinking Summary

Recruiter-ready narrative of how I used AI vibe-coding tools, product critique, and fast iteration to build a stronger claim-chart refinement prototype.

Core message: I treated the prototype as a product iteration challenge, not just a coding task. I used AI tools for build speed, but the product direction, workflow decisions, and quality bar were mine.

1. Recruiter Summary

For the Lumenci Associate Product Manager assignment, I used the prototype to demonstrate more than execution speed. I started with the fastest possible proof of concept in Streamlit to validate the core upload-chat-refine loop, then made a product judgment call that the experience felt too generic for a conversation-heavy legal workflow. I rebuilt it as a custom FastAPI plus HTML/CSS/JavaScript workspace so I could shape the interaction around analyst trust, row-level review, evidence defensibility, and recoverability.

The final result is stronger than my first pass because it reflects multiple rounds of critique and improvement. It now supports document intake, row selection, conversational refinement, source-backed suggestions, a dedicated review drawer, accept/reject/modify decisions, undo and version logging, evidence expansion when support is weak, and export-ready outputs. In short, it shows how I think about conversational AI products under ambiguity: build quickly, evaluate honestly, then iterate until the workflow actually fits the user.

2. Versioned Product Journey

Version What I changed Why I changed it Product outcome
v0 Built the first working prototype in Streamlit. I wanted to validate the upload, chat refinement, accept/reject, and export loop as quickly as possible. Fast proof of concept, but the product felt too dashboard-like and not tailored enough for a legal-analysis workflow.
v1 Moved to a custom FastAPI plus HTML/CSS/JavaScript application. Streamlit limited layout control and made it harder to create a purpose-built analyst workspace. Gained control over workflow framing, review surfaces, export patterns, and the overall product narrative.
v1.1 Added a guided workflow rail and a structured claim-chart workspace. I wanted the product to teach the workflow and reduce blank-screen uncertainty. Better onboarding, clearer next steps, and a stronger assignment story.
v1.2 Introduced a dedicated review drawer with accept, reject, and modify decisions. AI output should not silently overwrite a legal work product. Created a stronger human-in-the-loop pattern and lowered trust risk.
v1.3 Added version history, undo, and export-ready reference surfaces. Analysts need recoverability and auditability if AI gets something wrong. Made the prototype feel safer and more credible for legal review work.
v1.4 Added supporting-document append and URL ingestion during review. The model needed a productive recovery path when evidence was missing or weak. Handled the assignment edge case where AI cannot find enough evidence.
v1.5 Reworked metrics, diff presentation, responsive layout, and review-panel behavior. The prototype needed to look and behave like a product, not a raw AI output dump. Cleaner review experience, better information hierarchy, and stronger recruiter-facing polish.

4. Product Decisions That Show My Thinking

I chose not to make prompt engineering the user experience. Instead of relying on raw system-prompt exposure, I encoded guidance into the product through workflow steps, quick actions, review states, and structured surfaces. My belief was that patent analysts should not need to think like prompt engineers to get value from the product.

I treated AI as a collaborator that must be reviewed. In legal-analysis contexts, wrong evidence and weak reasoning are costly, so I made review, traceability, and approval central to the flow rather than optional.

I designed for failure and recovery, not just the happy path. Missing evidence, wrong evidence, undo, and follow-up refinement were all built in because conversational AI products become useful only when users can recover quickly from imperfect output.

5. Edge Cases and Design Problems I Solved While Building

Issue I identified Why it mattered What I changed
AI gives wrong evidence Blind trust would be risky in a legal workflow. Added source-backed review, citations, and accept/reject/modify loops so the analyst can inspect and correct the output before it updates the chart.
User wants to undo a previous refinement Analysts need a fast recovery path from a bad accepted suggestion. Added version history plus undo for the last accepted change.
AI cannot find strong evidence The workflow should continue productively even when support is incomplete. Added supporting-document append and URL ingestion so the model can ask for more evidence and continue.
Streamlit felt too generic The initial stack limited the product tone and layout control. Rebuilt the prototype as a custom web app with a more intentional analyst workspace.
Guided flow took too much space after onboarding Helpful onboarding should not permanently compete with the main work area. Kept it vertical on the left, made the width more responsive, and added hide/unhide behavior.
Metrics looked outdated Old gauge styling made the product feel less modern and less credible. Replaced older gauge-like visuals with cleaner live metric rings.
Chat and review competed inside one box Long conversations made review awkward and hard to scan. Separated the assistant chat from the review drawer so review does not get buried under chat history.
Sticky side panels collided with lower content Important sections were being visually covered or ending awkwardly. Removed the layout behaviors that caused collisions near the Reference Center and rebalanced the full-page flow.
Diff view looked like raw developer output Analysts and recruiters should not have to parse patch metadata. Converted the experience into clear "Removed Wording" and "Added Wording" review cards.
Review drawer did not adapt well to narrow width Compressed tabs, metrics, and cards weakened readability. Made the review drawer components responsive to the drawer itself instead of assuming full-page width.

6. What This Demonstrates About My Use of AI Tools

I did not use AI as a substitute for product thinking. I used it as a force multiplier. The important part was not just building quickly. The important part was recognizing when the first output was not good enough, converting vague discomfort into concrete product requirements, re-scoping the architecture when needed, and continuing to refine until the prototype better matched the actual user context.

This is the signal I would want a recruiter to see: I can use AI tools to accelerate execution while still owning judgment, prioritization, workflow design, and quality control.
