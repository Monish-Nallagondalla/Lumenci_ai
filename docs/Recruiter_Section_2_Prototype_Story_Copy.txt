PROTOTYPE EVOLUTION, PRODUCT THINKING, AND WHAT I ACHIEVED

RECRUITER SUMMARY

For the Lumenci Associate Product Manager assignment, I treated the prototype as more than a coding exercise. I used AI vibe-coding tools to accelerate execution, but I did not mistake speed for product quality. I started with a Streamlit prototype because it was the fastest way to validate the core loop: upload a claim chart, refine rows conversationally, review AI output, and export the result. Once I validated that flow, I made a deliberate product decision to move away from Streamlit and rebuild the experience as a custom FastAPI + HTML/CSS/JavaScript workspace.

I made that change because the problem was not just "show chat next to a table." This is a high-consequence legal-analysis workflow where weak evidence, vague reasoning, or silent AI errors can create real cost. I wanted the product to feel purpose-built for patent analysts, not like a generic demo dashboard. That shift allowed me to design a more opinionated analyst workflow with guided onboarding, row-level refinement, source-backed review, version control, undo, and export surfaces.

Most importantly, I designed the product around a core belief: AI systems are probabilistic and non-deterministic, especially in evidence-heavy legal contexts. Because failure is costly and product teams can be held responsible when users over-trust AI output, I intentionally chose workflows that move the experience as close as possible to fail-safe behavior. Instead of optimizing for "one-click magic," I optimized for inspectability, human review, recovery, and controlled application of change.

WHAT I BUILT IN THE FINAL PROTOTYPE

- Start the workflow through either sample mode or document upload
- Display a structured three-column claim chart workspace
- Highlight weak evidence and vague reasoning at the row level
- Let the analyst select a claim row and request refinement through chat or quick actions
- Retrieve relevant support snippets before generating a recommendation
- Present AI output in a dedicated review drawer with evidence changes, reasoning changes, citations, scrutiny indicators, and challenge-readiness prompts
- Require analyst approval before any change is applied to the chart
- Support accept, reject, modify-via-chat, undo last accepted change, and version logging
- Allow evidence expansion mid-workflow through appended support documents or URL ingestion
- Export the refined chart and supporting outputs for downstream legal use

KEY PRODUCT DECISIONS THAT SHOW MY THINKING

1. I designed for legal trust, not just AI novelty.
In this problem space, a weak suggestion is not a harmless model miss. If unsupported reasoning is accepted too easily, the product can actively create bad downstream work. That is why I designed the workflow so AI suggestions are visible, challengeable, source-backed, and reversible before they become part of the chart.

2. I chose fail-safe workflow patterns because AI is non-deterministic.
I understand that LLM-based systems are inherently probabilistic. They can produce good outputs many times in a row and still fail in subtle ways on the next turn. In a legal-analysis workflow, product owners cannot treat that as a small UX issue. They can be held responsible if the system encourages over-trust in unsupported output. My response was to design for near fail-safe behavior through explicit review, citations, versioning, undo, and recovery paths rather than pretending the model will always be right.

3. I reduced prompt burden by embedding guidance into the product.
The assignment mentions system prompts and conversational interaction. I decided the best experience was not to make analysts behave like prompt engineers. Instead, I encoded guidance into the interface itself through workflow rails, row states, quick actions, review tabs, and structured decision points.

4. I optimized for graceful recovery, not only for the happy path.
The product is useful only if users can recover quickly when the model is wrong, vague, or blocked by weak evidence. That is why wrong-evidence correction, undo, modify-via-chat, and evidence-expansion flows became first-class parts of the design.
